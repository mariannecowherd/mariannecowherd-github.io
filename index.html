What I Do

I study the role of wildfire management, within the context of changing climate, on hydrology and vegetation of the mountainous watersheds in the Sierra Nevada. The results of my research will aide watershed management decisions with the goal of minimizing catastrophic fires, promoting landscape diversity, and increasing water yield .

Wildfires Have Different Impacts on Two Watersheds

Findings: Over 47 years of wildfires has little impacts on vegetation and soil moisture of one basin but has great impacts on another basin.

We used satellite imagery to quantify vegetation transitions (Figure 1) during the 47 years of managed wildfire in SCB (1971-2018) and compared these transitions to the changes observed in ICB (Boisramé et al, 2018).

Figure 1: Vegetation transitions throughout wildfire management in SCB (top row) and SCB (bottom row).

Both spatially distributed and in situ soil moisture data were collected in SCB and ICB and categorized by different vegetation types (mixed conifer, dense meadow, sparse meadow, and shrub). Dense meadows had the highest soil moisture values, but there was little difference between all other cover types in SCB (Figure 2).

Figure 2: Distribution of soil moisture (in terms of volumetric water content) for each site-date-vegetation class combination in SCB.

Fire-driven changes in dominant vegetation type (observed from aerial imagery analysis) and forest structure (observed from forestry plot data) were minimal at SCB, especially when compared to ICB (Figure 3).

Figure 3: Percent of the total vegetated area covered by each vegetation class for both Illilouette Creek Basin (ICB) and Sugarloaf Creek Basin (SCB).

This can be partially explained by SCB's lower productivity (Figure 4) associated with lower precipitation totals (56-88% of ICB's precipitation). We infer that the managed fire regime in SCB has not led to large changes in soil moisture regimes in the basin.

Figure 4: Normalized difference vegetation index (NDVI; averaged across a given basin for a given date), a proxy for productivity, was consistently higher in Illilouette Creek Basin than Sugarloaf Creek Basin.

Hydrological benefits of historically observed wildfire management on a medium sized watershed are expected to have similar effects in future climate.

The hydrological effects of restoring wildfire in Sierra Nevada forests are of great interest to managers, given the importance of these forests to California's water supply. Data analysis and past modeling studies of the Illilouette Creek Basin (ICB) found that managed wildfire increased streamflow yields from the basin by approximately 5% compared to during fire suppression, and did so during a period when streamflow in other regional basins was declining (Boisramé et al. 2017, Boisramé et al. 2019). However, the changes in the ICB fire management policy were initiated nearly 50 years ago. Fire management changes implemented now and over the coming years in Sierra Nevada basins will occur under a different climate and fire regime than what was experienced during the 20th Century (Figure 1).

Figure 1: The historical modeling time period is from 1972 to 2012 (A), denoting observed 40 years since the end of fire suppression policies in ICB. Future modeling time period is from 2030 to 2070 (B) and two climate scenarios are used. Blue, orange, and red vertical lines denote historical, 30%, and 60% increase in frequency of fire events, respectively.

Figure 2 (right hand panel) shows how basin-scale streamflow and soil moisture storage change following the restoration of a fire regime under historical, rcp 4.5, and rcp 8.5 climate scenarios. The results indicate that despite minor differences in the timeseries, broadly there is no difference in the impact of fire on ICB hydrology under future versus historical climates: in both cases, restoring fire regimes in the basin leads to an increase of 15 mm/year specific discharge and a maximum increase of 32-33 mm soil water storage. This streamflow increase equates to ~$1.5-2.3 million of hydropower revenue and $6.0-9.2 million in water sales per year.

Figure 2: Modeling results of streamflow and soil moisture changes; positive change indicatesa relative increase in the variable as a result of fires. Left panel shows results for different fire frequency regimes under rcp 4.5 climate and right panel shows historical fire frequencysimulations under three different climate scenarios. Shaded areas indicate the 95% confidenceinterval for the simulations. Vertical orange lines represent timing of observed historical fires.
